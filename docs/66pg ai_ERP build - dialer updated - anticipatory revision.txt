Dominion Master Strategy Document
main goal is to build Dominion Home Deals into a lean, AI-leveraged real estate wholesaling company that generates about $2 million a year with just a 2-man team.
In practical terms, that means creating a system where technology and AI multiply your speed, consistency, lead handling, follow-up, and deal conversion so the business can perform like a much larger operation without needing a large staff.
CRM, intelligence layer, AI operations, labor compression, lead-finding, trust, and moat plan
This document is a full capture and synthesis of the ideas, plans, cautions, architecture decisions, and open questions discussed in this conversation. It is written to preserve strategic value, not to compress it away.
1. Executive overview
Who we are
Dominion Home Deals is a very small, highly committed wholesaling / wholetail / novation business. Adam is the backend and systems operator: bookkeeping, Google Ads, KPI ownership, and CRM buildout. Logan is the primary acquisitions and dispositions operator: calling sellers, handling inbound, running acquisitions, and maintaining buyer relationships. Spokane County is the primary market, with Kootenai as an adjacent secondary market.
Our current stage
The business is early. There is live marketing, a basic live CRM, a small buyer network, and strong intent, but the sales machine is not yet mature. Current workflow weaknesses include no written intake script, weak qualification discipline, no real stage system, leads living mostly in emails or paper notes, weekly KPI review not yet established, offer confidence still developing, and only about 5% of leads becoming actual offers.
Our goals
The near-term goal is 12 deals in 90 days, with a minimum acceptable assignment fee of $12,000. The business needs income fast, but the long-term goal is a durable machine. Adam wants the business to become strong enough that he can go full time.
Our constraints
Cash available is about $5,000. Spokane is the main focus. Best niches are inherited homes with tax problems and absentee landlords. Outbound is call-only in Washington. The business refuses to outsource cold calling. Trust matters because sellers may fear fraud and may suspect wholesaling is shady.
Why this strategy matters
A company this small cannot win by copying the overhead model of bigger players. It has to win by compressing support labor, understanding fewer leads better, and building a system that makes one strong operator dramatically more effective.
Core thesis of the entire conversation
The main thesis is:
Dominion should become a small, human-trust business with an unusually strong AI operations and intelligence layer.
Not a bloated ERP.
 Not a basic CRM with novelty AI tricks.
 Not an autonomous robo-wholesaler.
The real edge is:
better lead understanding per seller-minute

lower support-labor overhead

stronger trust in conversation

tighter workflow discipline

more signal from messy local data

a compounding local memory and feedback loop

Why fewer better-understood leads can beat more generic leads
Big players often optimize for more volume and more labor. Dominion’s better path is:

better understood

better timed

That fits inherited, absentee, and burden-driven seller types much better than brute-force list blasting.

6. Architecture plan
Why the intelligence layer should be separate conceptually from the CRM
Because CRM and intelligence do different jobs.
CRM:
move work

assign actions

store operator-ready state

keep pipeline clean

Intelligence:
gather messy evidence

resolve identity

enrich

infer cautiously

score

explain

learn

If those are mixed too early, the CRM becomes brittle, cluttered, and hard to trust.
Same-repo / same-db first vs separate service later
Because there is already a live Sentinel system, the right path is:
same repo first

same database first

bounded context first

physical separation later if justified

This avoids premature enterprise design while still preserving architectural sanity.

This should remain the dominant architectural frame:
Sentinel = action

intelligence domain = understanding

Dialer strategy update
The dialer should be treated as a conversation operating system. That means the product is no longer just telephony plus notes; it is the live workspace where context, memory, AI assistance, routing, and post-call discipline come together before curated outputs are promoted into Sentinel.
• Main standard: evaluate every dialer or voice feature by contracts per founder-hour, speed-to-lead, follow-up reliability, and cleaner conversion workflow.
• Keep the human as the trust-heavy closer, but use AI aggressively before, during, and after calls where it compresses labor or improves consistency.
• The correct product split is AI front office plus AI back office: receptionist, switchboard, missed-call recovery, callback booking, and warm transfer on the front end; notes, summaries, extracted facts, objection tagging, follow-up drafting, QA, and stale-lead detection on the back end.
• The dialer should own volatile call-session state. Sentinel should own durable lead and property truth. Only confirmed or operator-approved outputs should be published back into the client file.
Additional dialer capabilities to prioritize
• Conversation-native STT with keyterm boosting and interruption-aware turn handling, not just raw transcript capture.
• Seller-memory panel: last three summaries, unresolved objections, promised follow-up, likely decision-maker, best callback time, and known sensitivities.
• Prompt-cached context stack: standard domain playbook, local trust language, compliance language, and dynamic lead facts delivered in a repeatable format.
• Agent observability and evals: transfer traces, summary quality review, latency, routing success, and prompt-version comparisons.
• Tool-enabled voice actions: transfer, SMS, callback booking, API requests, and lead/session creation triggered from voice workflows.
• Call QA and objection intelligence that feeds scripts, follow-up logic, and list/source feedback loops.
• Selective outbound AI screening later as a measured experiment, not a first principle and not the main strategy.
Reframed voice position
Inbound and warm transfer remain the highest-confidence first voice edge because they save labor quickly and protect trust. But the real strategic rule is broader: use AI wherever it increases throughput enough to justify the complexity and risk. That means the system should preserve optionality for later outbound screening experiments once logging, evaluation, compliance controls, and session ownership are strong.

21. What is likely to change next
AI advantage will shift from smarter models to better operating stacks
The likely bottleneck will not be access to frontier models. The bottleneck will be agent runtime quality, tool connectivity, traceability, prompt discipline, evaluation loops, and safe deployment. Dominion should therefore treat AI as an operational stack, not just a set of features.
Voice will improve fast, but trust, carrier reputation, and compliance will matter even more
AI voice quality, latency, and tool use will keep improving. That does not mean autonomous calling becomes the best strategy. The likely winners will have better routing, better warm transfer, better fallback behavior, better approved language, better consent and source discipline, and cleaner human takeover rules.
Seller discovery will become more conversational and AI-mediated
Search engines and answer engines are moving toward conversational summaries, follow-up questions, and trust-signal synthesis. Dominion should assume some future sellers will evaluate local cash buyers through AI-generated explanations before they ever click an ad or a blue link.
Generic receptionist, note-taking, and basic lead-scoring tools will commoditize
More operators will have access to inbound AI answering, transcription, summaries, and generic enrichment. Those tools will matter, but they will not be enough to create durable edge by themselves.
The durable moat will be local memory, buyer liquidity, trust conversion, and timing loops
Dominion's future edge is most likely to come from proprietary local memory of people, properties, burdens, buyers, timing changes, seller objections, and outcomes. Timing and attention allocation may become more valuable than static enrichment alone.
Browser and computer-use research will often beat custom integrations early
Messy portals, county pages, document viewers, and fragmented public sources are unlikely to become simpler. A bounded browser research runner with provenance and review is likely to provide faster leverage than overbuilding brittle custom scrapers too early.
